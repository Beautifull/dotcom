Hebrew Anniversaries: Yahrzeits and Birthdays

How does Hebcal determine an anniversary occurring in Adar?

Hebcal uses the algorithm defined in Calendrical Calculations by Edward M. Reingold and Nachum Dershowitz. The authors write (p. 111):

The birthday of someone born in Adar of an ordinary year or Adar II of a leap year is also always in the last month of the year, be that Adar or Adar II. Someone born on the thirtieth day of Cheshvan, Kislev, or Adar I has his birthday postponed until the first of the following month in years where that day does not occur.

The rule for a Yahrzeit is similar (p. 113):

The customary anniversary date of a death is more complicated and depends also on the character of the year in which the first anniversary occurs. There are several cases:
  1. If the date of death is Cheshvan 30, the anniversary in general depends on the first anniversary; if that first anniversary was not Chesvan 30, use the day before Kislev 1.
  2. If the date of death is Kislev 30, the anniverary in general again depends on the first anniverary -- if that was not Kislev 30, use the day before Tevet 1.
  3. If the date of death is Adar II, the anniversary is the same day in the last month of the Hebrew year (Adar or Adar II).
  4. If the date of death is Adar I 30, the anniversary in a Hebrew year that is not a leap year (in which Adar only has 29 days) is the last day in Shevat.
  5. In all other cases, use the normal (birthday) anniverary of the date of death.

On page 114, they write:

There are minor variations in custom regarding the anniversary date in some of these cases. For example, Spanish and Portuguese Jews never observe the anniversary in Adar I.

Lastly, a note from the Hebcal authors: there are undoubtedly many differing opinions regarding when to observe an Adar yahrzeit. For all matters of halacha, consult your local rabbi.
